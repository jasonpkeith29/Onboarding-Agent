Phase 4 — Layer 3 DEP Onboarding
Month 3+

Operate: Full Role Accountability

The scaffolding comes down. Phase 4 is not a new set of tasks to complete — it's a confirmation that you're operating as a full PM without prompting. You own your roadmap. You run your own readiness signoffs. You close the loop on shipped work. You socialize your strategy without being asked. At month 3, the question is no longer "are you learning the role?" It's "are you doing it?"

What changes at month 3

In phases 1-3, your manager and the onboarding program provide structure. Phase 4 is when you internalize that structure and operate from it. The test is not whether you can answer the right questions when asked — it's whether you're asking them yourself before anyone prompts you.

What full accountability looks like
Roadmap Ownership

Without prompting

Roadmap inputs are current before PrOps asks. Backlog order reflects judgment, not accumulation. You know where the next three priorities sit and why — and you can explain it to a new stakeholder without slides.

If PrOps is chasing you for Jira hygiene at month 3, that's a gap.

Release Readiness

PM-owned, not deferred

You lead product readiness sign-off for your releases. This is separate from technical readiness — it's your call, not engineering's. Alpha/beta exit criteria are clear before rollout decisions are made.

Product readiness is a PM accountability. Not deferring it to PrOps or engineering is the bar.

Post-Launch Learning

Closing the loop

You complete a post-launch review for a shipped feature and share the learning — not because it's required, but because you know what you expected to happen and you want to understand why the result was different.

The post-launch review is the most important PM habit most PMs skip. Do it anyway.

Stakeholder Engagement

Proactive, not reactive

You regularly socialize your area's strategic direction without being asked. Key stakeholders know where your roadmap is headed before they encounter your work in a Zone Review or a launch announcement.

If stakeholders are surprised by your decisions at month 3, alignment is not happening proactively.

Zone Review Contribution

Present or contribute

You present or materially contribute to a Zone Review or Release Preview for your product area. You know what data leadership is looking at for your area and you bring it without being reminded.

Zone Review presence is a credibility signal. Show up with data, not summaries of activity.

Strategy Articulation

Defensible in any conversation

You can explain the vision for your area clearly and specifically — where it's headed, why it matters, how current work connects to that direction — to any stakeholder, at any time, without notes.

A product manager who can't explain their area's direction without a slide deck is not operating strategically.

The 6-month close

How the program ends — and what comes from it

At the 6-month mark, both you and your manager independently complete a closing reflection. You'll answer questions about what you're most proud of, where you want honest feedback, and what you'd change about the program.

Your manager will assess you on independent ownership, stakeholder communication, and strategic clarity — and rate each area on a 1-5 scale against what you've shown.

The agent surfaces both assessments, flags where you aligned and where you diverged, and generates a closing report. The divergences become the agenda for a closing 1:1 — a conversation you should have with your manager, not skip because the program is ending.

6-Month Closing Report includes:
  • Domain ratings: independent ownership, stakeholder communication, strategic clarity
  • Competency assessment across all six competencies
  • Divergence analysis — where manager and new hire reads differ
  • Development priorities for the next 6 months
  • Complete list of program artifacts produced
  • Feedback from both parties on the program itself

The divergences in the closing report are your most valuable data. Where you and your manager see things differently is where the most important growth conversations live.

What your manager is watching for — by level

Manager watch-fors in Phase 4

Associate
  • Is the new hire making the transition from task-executor to outcome-owner?
  • Are they tracking whether their work is achieving its intended outcome?
  • Are they managing their own work without constant check-ins?
Sr. Associate
  • Are they owning sprint-level execution end-to-end without prompting?
  • Are they completing post-launch reviews for assigned features?
  • Are they contributing to roadmap discussions, not just attending them?
Manager
  • Is the PM operating independently — owning their area without prompting?
  • Is the post-launch review producing real behavior change?
  • Are they socializing their strategy proactively, or waiting to present in formal settings?
Sr. Manager
  • Is the Sr. Manager operating at a zone level — thinking about cross-cutting concerns, not just their own area?
  • Are they developing junior PMs on the team?
  • Are they contributing to zone-level decisions and strategy?
Director / ED
  • Is the Director owning outcomes across the entire zone?
  • Are they engaging at ELT level credibly?
  • Are they building a team and setting the standard for PM quality zone-wide?
Phase 4 milestones

What should be true at month 3 and at 6 months

  • Own your roadmap inputs end-to-end without PrOps reminders — Jira fields are current before anyone asks
  • Lead product readiness sign-off for a release, separately from technical readiness
  • Complete a post-launch review for a shipped feature and share the learning with the team and manager
  • Regularly socialize your area's strategic direction with stakeholders across the business — proactively
  • Present or materially contribute to a Zone Review or Release Preview for your product area
  • Complete the Phase 4 reflection and 6-month closing in the Claude Code agent — generate the closing report
