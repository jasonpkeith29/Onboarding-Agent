The DEP Zone PM Role

What Product Management Means in DEP @ Athena

Before you look at processes, tools, or ceremonies — start here. This page defines what product management actually is at athena, what it specifically means in this zone, and where the boundaries of the role sit.

North Star

We exist to make our stakeholders unstoppable

As a platform team, our mission is to build the data, pipeline, reporting, and analytics capabilities that allow every internal and external partner to do their best work — faster, smarter, and with greater confidence.

Where DEP fits — what we do, how we operate, and who we support

DEP Data & Ecosystem in one sentence

DEP Data & Ecosystem exists to make athena's data, connectivity, and analytics capabilities into reusable platform assets that accelerate onboarding, power workflows, enable partners, and turn data into customer and business value.

Four capability areas
Data Services

Liberating and standardizing data across networks to power onboarding, migration, population health, VBC, and analytics. Covers imports, migrations, external data pipelines, network data, and shared data assets.

Open Ecosystem

Making athena an open platform where customers, partners, and developers can build and securely run integrated solutions. Covers marketplace, athenaConnect, APIs, identity/access, and external integrations.

Analytics Platform

Giving customers actionable insights that improve financial, operational, and clinical performance. Covers Data View, reporting, dashboards, analytics delivery, and analytics infrastructure.

Integration Platform

Reducing manual integration work through self-service, monitoring, automation, and athena-aware tooling. Covers interfaces, EDI, faxing, onboarding, monitoring, self-service tooling, and error automation.

How DEP supports the business
Growth

Faster onboarding, easier migrations, marketplace and partner revenue, payer/network products, and sales-demo enablement.

Retention & Customer Value

Better reporting, better analytics, better data availability, and smoother workflows inside athenaOne.

Operational Scale

Automation, self-service, lower turnaround times, fewer manual build tasks, and fewer support cases.

Risk & Platform Durability

HITRUST/compliance, data security, patient matching and patient safety, infrastructure modernization, and cost discipline.

The PM mandate — three things that never change regardless of level
Define

WHAT to build and WHY

PMs define the vision for their area, translate it into a roadmap, and ensure the team always understands the business problem being solved — not just the feature being built.

Steward

R&D investment like a financial manager

PMs are accountable for the business outcomes of their products and the return on our R&D investment. Every prioritization decision is a capital allocation decision.

Drive

Outcomes, not outputs

Features, releases, and launches are outputs. Behaviors, unit economics, adoption, and efficiency gains are outcomes. Shipping is not success. Impact is success.

Product management vs. agile process — not the same thing
Product Management

The WHAT — PM leads this

  • Vision, strategy, and long-term direction
  • Roadmap and prioritization rationale
  • Customer and stakeholder research
  • Problem definition and hypothesis generation
  • Value, viability, usability, feasibility
  • Unit economics and outcome measurement
≠
Agile Process

The HOW — Engineering leads this

  • Backlog management and sprint planning
  • Grooming and velocity tracking
  • Testing, deploying, and delivery mechanics
  • Technical approach and architecture
  • Release cadence and sprint ceremonies

Internal customer? Doesn't matter — it's the same job.

Even for our most internal and technical products, the role of PM is still fundamentally to manage the outcomes of the R&D investment. For platform teams, the economics PMs track are operational: unit costs, efficiency rates, required touches, time on task, and NPS/TAT measures.

What PM owns — and what it shares with partners
Product Manager

Owns the vision, the why, and the what

  • Strategic vision for the product area
  • Short- and long-term roadmap direction
  • Problem clarity and customer context
  • Prioritization rationale and roadmap integrity
  • Artifact quality — PRDs, epics, stories, AC
  • Product readiness at launch
  • Success metrics and post-launch accountability
  • Stakeholder communication and alignment
Engineering

Owns the how and the when

  • Technical feasibility and sequencing
  • Implementation quality and technical debt
  • Technical risk visibility and readiness
  • Root cause and resolution for defects
  • Delivery estimates and dependency realities
Architecture

Owns the technical direction

  • Overall architecture assessment and design
  • Evaluation of flows, pipelines, and technology paths
  • Recommendations on build vs. buy vs. integrate decisions
  • Technical standards and platform consistency across areas
  • Risk identification for architectural choices
  • Cross-area dependency and integration design
Product Ops

Owns the operating rhythm

  • Cadence, ceremonies, and milestone coordination
  • Roadmap tooling, publication, and hygiene
  • Release coordination and enablement
  • Alpha/beta logistics and feedback loops
  • Cross-functional visibility and follow-through
UX / Design

Owns the experience quality

  • User research and problem validation
  • Interaction design and usability
  • Content, helper text, and narrative clarity
  • UX review and sign-off before broader rollout
Competencies & Expectations

Six competencies. Five levels. One bar that rises over time.

These are the six competencies expected of Product Managers in the DEP zone across every level from Associate to Executive Director. Use the level selector to see what each competency looks like at your level, then scroll down for the full career framework.

A note on this zone's bar

The company-wide framework positions strategic thinking as a skill that deepens at Senior Manager. In this zone, we expect PMs to begin developing strategic thinking from day one — because as a platform team, if PMs aren't defining clear product vision and aligning stakeholders around it, no one else will.

View level:
Competency 1

Curiosity

Actively seeks to understand customers, workflows, pain points, stakeholders, systems, and market context by asking strong questions and investigating assumptions before making decisions.

Associate

Asks questions and gathers information as directed. Developing the habit of checking assumptions before accepting requests at face value. Learning to distinguish between what a stakeholder asks for and what they actually need.

Sr. Associate

Investigates independently within a defined scope. Pulls from multiple sources rather than relying on what's handed to them. Begins forming their own views on what the problem is before solution work begins.

What good looks like

Pulls signal from multiple sources, probes for root causes, keeps learning even after an initial answer appears obvious. Asks strong questions before entering solutioning.

Watch out for

Accepting surface-level requests at face value or moving into solutioning before the problem is fully understood.

Sr. Manager

Generates new insight for the product area. Identifies questions the team hasn't asked yet. Leads thinking on where the product should go based on deep synthesis of customer, market, and competitive signal.

Director / ED

Drives curiosity as an organizational practice. Builds teams that investigate rather than assume. Synthesizes signal from across the industry and customer base to set strategic direction.

Competency 2

Strategic Thinking

Defines a clear long-term direction for the product area and translates ambiguity into a coherent vision, roadmap, and set of investment choices tied to business and customer outcomes.

Associate

Understands the concept of product vision but primarily follows direction set by others. Focused on understanding why the current roadmap is prioritized the way it is.

Sr. Associate

Begins to connect sprint-level work to the broader roadmap. Can explain what the current release is trying to achieve and why specific features were prioritized.

What good looks like

Can explain where the area is headed, why it matters, and how current work ladders into that direction. Creates the roadmap and manages what's in and out each release.

Watch out for

Managing a list of requests or releases without a durable product narrative or a clear logic for what moves first.

Sr. Manager

Interprets zone and division OKRs into realistic choices for the team. Defines 1–3 year direction. Identifies gotchas ahead of zone leads. Moves from executing the plan to shaping the strategy.

Director / ED

Owns the 3–5 year vision for the zone. Translates corporate strategy into a multi-year roadmap. Engages at ELT level to influence strategy, not just report on it.

Competency 3

Influence

Aligns teams and stakeholders without formal authority by listening well, building trust, framing tradeoffs clearly, and communicating in ways that move people toward shared decisions and action.

Associate

Communicates clearly within the immediate team. Developing reliability as the foundation of early credibility.

Sr. Associate

Builds working relationships with the scrum team. Earns credibility through consistent preparation and follow-through rather than authority.

What good looks like

Earns buy-in across engineering, UX, leadership, and partner teams. Can move a decision forward without relying on escalation by default.

Watch out for

Repeating updates without actually changing alignment, or waiting for authority from others to create momentum.

Sr. Manager

Influences across zone and sub-division. Executive-level communication and presentation. Leads cross-functional initiatives without direct authority.

Director / ED

Independently manages ELT-level conversations to secure decisions. Negotiates and manages relationships with clients and prospects. Moves athena as an organization.

Competency 4

Customer & Market Insight

Represents the voice of the customer and the realities of the market by grounding decisions in research, workflow understanding, competitive context, and direct feedback.

Associate

Consumes research and customer context provided by others. Learning to ask good questions about who the stakeholder is and what workflow they're in.

Sr. Associate

Begins gathering direct signal through observation. Can describe the primary stakeholder and their core workflow for assigned areas.

What good looks like

Can explain who the stakeholder is, what workflow they're in, what pain point exists, and why the problem matters now. Owns 1–2 key measures.

Watch out for

Talking mostly in internal terms or being unable to clearly connect work to real workflow friction.

Sr. Manager

Primary POC for clients, partners, and vendors. Owns a portfolio of 5–10 measures. Industry awareness and competitive landscape shape roadmap and strategy.

Director / ED

Deep awareness of industry trends, market shifts, and competitor activity. Synthesizes the voice of the market into a multi-year product direction.

Competency 5

Prioritization & Judgment

Makes sound tradeoff decisions using evidence, context, and clear reasoning; distinguishes urgent from important; and sequences work to balance value, constraints, and long-term health.

Associate

Follows established prioritization logic. Learning to distinguish between urgent and important requests with guidance.

Sr. Associate

Applies RICE and MoSCoW scoring within a defined scope. Begins to make and articulate small tradeoff decisions independently.

What good looks like

Uses explicit prioritization logic (RICE + MoSCoW), can explain tradeoffs, and keeps the backlog ordered in a way others can follow.

Watch out for

Prioritizing reactively or letting backlog order reflect noise and stakeholder volume rather than judgment.

Sr. Manager

Judicious on go/no-go decisions. Widens scope to discovery through sunset. Moves to economic ownership — understanding cost and return implications of prioritization decisions.

Director / ED

Makes strategic-level tradeoffs across the entire zone portfolio. Balances delivery commitments against long-term platform health and market positioning.

Competency 6

Execution & Outcome Ownership

Turns strategy into clear product artifacts, decisions, and delivery guidance; maintains quality through launch; and stays accountable for whether shipped work produces the intended outcome.

Associate

Completes defined tasks reliably. Learning what "done" actually means beyond code complete — acceptance criteria, readiness, and customer impact.

Sr. Associate

Owns sprint-level delivery for assigned stories. Begins closing the loop by checking whether shipped work achieved its intended outcome.

What good looks like

Provides clear artifacts, supports smooth engineering elaboration, owns product readiness separately from technical readiness, and closes the loop with post-launch learning.

Watch out for

Stopping at documentation or treating launch as the finish line.

Sr. Manager

Anticipates leadership needs proactively. Takes responsibility for cross-functional org initiatives. Evangelizes product outcomes when proven.

Director / ED

Accountable for outcomes across the entire zone. Defines and tracks the zone scorecard. Connects outcome data to ELT and commercial conversations.

Three things that drive promotion
1

Role — Perform

Demonstrate the ability to play the role of the next level while continuing strong performance at your current level. This is about operating at a higher level of accountability and judgment in real situations.

2

Scope — Talk

Work with your manager to identify opportunities to operate at next-level scope. Seek vacuums — find areas where the team or company is struggling and pitch in. These are the highest-leverage opportunities to demonstrate next-level skills.

3

Skills — Practice

Apply the 80/20 rule: focus on the 20% of contributions that demonstrate 80% of your readiness for the next level. Seek feedback deliberately, apply it in your work, and move toward your strengths over time.

Strategic Planning

Strategic Planning

PM sets high-level product direction — defining the vision, connecting it to business outcomes, and building the roadmap that guides all downstream work.

Who owns what
  • PM — Product vision, strategic roadmap, problem definition, prioritization rationale, stakeholder alignment
  • Engineering — Technical feasibility signals, architecture direction, dependency identification
  • Architecture — Overall architecture assessment, evaluation of technology paths and flows, recommendations on build vs. buy vs. integrate decisions, technical standards
  • Product Ops — New service and product GTM process planning support, cross-R&D initiative tracking
  • UX — User research, market insight, experience strategy
PM focus at your level
  • Observe how product vision and roadmap direction are set — attend planning discussions and take notes
  • Ask questions to understand why certain strategic bets are being made
  • Support research and synthesis tasks that inform strategic direction
  • Begin contributing to strategic discussions with data and customer context
  • Help draft problem statements and opportunity framing for assigned areas
  • Learn how boulders and OKRs cascade into team-level roadmap choices
  • Define and evangelize a clear vision for your product area
  • Translate boulders and OKRs into a short- and long-term roadmap
  • Make prioritization rationale visible using RICE + MoSCoW scoring
  • Align stakeholders around customer workflows and pain points before roadmap commitments are made
  • Surface tradeoffs and scope changes before review windows
  • Interpret zone and division OKRs into realistic strategic choices for the team
  • Challenge whether the right problems are being prioritized — push back on weak strategic framing
  • Make go/no-go decisions with visible economic rationale, not just product logic
  • Own the 3-year roadmap direction for the zone
  • Connect roadmap choices to market positioning and corporate strategy
  • Represent the product direction externally to clients, ELT, and commercial partners
  • Ensure every PM in the zone has a visible, defensible strategic narrative for their area
What your manager is watching for
  • Are you developing the habit of asking why before moving to solutions?
  • Are you learning from strategic discussions, not just observing passively?
  • Can you explain the roadmap direction for your area and why it's prioritized the way it is?
  • Are your strategic contributions grounded in data and customer insight?
  • Are you developing your own point of view, not just summarizing what others say?
  • Can you connect your area's work to the broader zone strategy?
  • Is the PM consistently framing problems and vision before committing to roadmap items?
  • Is the strategic narrative visible and defensible to leadership and partner teams?
  • Is stakeholder alignment being built proactively, not reactively?
  • Is strategic direction reflecting zone-level ambition, not just team-level execution?
  • Are economic tradeoffs visible in prioritization decisions?
  • Is the Sr. Manager developing Manager-level PMs to own strategic framing independently?
  • Is the zone's strategic direction generating genuine market insight, not just feature planning?
  • Is the Director setting a zone-wide standard for strategic quality?
  • Is the product direction being communicated credibly to ELT and external stakeholders?

Discovery & Definition

Discovery & Definition

PM investigates and prioritizes user problems, establishes directional hypotheses, and defines work clearly enough for architecture and build to begin.

Who owns what
  • PM — Problem framing, requirements definition, artifact quality, PRDs, epics, stories, acceptance criteria
  • Engineering — Architecture direction, technical feasibility, dependency and complexity assessment
  • Architecture — Pipeline and flow design, technology path evaluation, integration patterns, platform consistency recommendations, technical risk identification
  • Product Ops — Roadmap/Jira tooling and PM execution best practices, scoping new Jira features
  • UX — User research, usability analysis, interaction design, prototype validation
PM focus at your level
  • Learn what makes a problem statement clear and evidence-backed
  • Shadow discovery conversations and help synthesize research findings
  • Begin writing user stories for assigned features with PM review before grooming
  • Draft problem statements and opportunity framing for assigned areas
  • Own story writing for assigned scope in sprint-ready condition
  • Begin writing acceptance criteria that engineering can execute without follow-up questions
  • Frame customer and business problems before solutioning begins
  • Write PRDs, epics, stories, and acceptance criteria to the zone quality bar
  • Ensure Jira fields are complete and accurate without being chased by PrOps
  • Define success metrics, dependencies, risks, and mitigation in every major artifact
  • Bring work into grooming in a form that is clear and complete for engineering
  • Set the artifact quality bar for the team — coach PMs whose work needs cleanup
  • Spot-check discovery quality and problem framing in planning and BURP sessions
  • Ensure hypotheses are directional and connected to real customer workflows before architecture begins
  • Own the zone-wide artifact quality standard — what does done look like for PRDs, epics, and stories?
  • Ensure discovery practice is rigorous across all areas, not just individual teams
  • Use AI tooling and templates to raise the floor for first-pass definition quality
What your manager is watching for
  • Are you distinguishing between what a stakeholder asks for and what they actually need?
  • Are your drafted stories improving in clarity without repeated correction?
  • Are you validating problems before jumping to solutions?
  • Is your work arriving at grooming in sprint-ready condition?
  • Are your acceptance criteria executable by engineering without follow-up questions?
  • Are you owning your assigned scope without requiring cleanup?
  • Is the PM's artifact quality enabling clean engineering elaboration, or creating rework cycles?
  • Is the PM being chased by PrOps for Jira hygiene, or proactively maintaining it?
  • Are discovery and definition happening before commitments are made, not after?
  • Is the Sr. Manager setting a clear quality standard for the team, not just for their own work?
  • Is discovery discipline improving across the team as a result of coaching?
  • Are hypotheses grounded in real customer evidence, not just internal assumptions?
  • Does the zone have a documented, consistent definition quality standard?
  • Is AI tooling being used to raise the floor across teams?
  • Is discovery quality improving as a zone-wide practice?

Execution

PM owns product readiness through build and launch — ensuring the team has clarity to deliver and customers and partners are ready before customer exposure begins.

Who owns what
  • PM — Product readiness, launch narrative, rollout intent, alpha/beta objectives and exit decisions
  • Engineering — Technical readiness, implementation quality, deployment, fix path
  • Architecture — Technical sign-off on architectural decisions made during build, validation that implementation aligns with intended design patterns and platform standards
  • Product Ops — Alpha/beta test planning, execution and data collection, GA readiness services
  • UX — Design validation, usability testing, experience signoff for customer-facing changes
PM focus at your level
  • Learn the difference between code deployment and customer rollout
  • Shadow the PM through TRA, release readiness signoff, and alpha/beta coordination
  • Understand what product readiness means and what PM specifically signs off on
  • Support launch inputs for assigned features — release notes, Jira readiness fields, feature flags
  • Begin participating in alpha/beta coordination with PrOps guidance
  • Learn the TRA process and what engineering vs. PM each own within it
  • Sign off on product readiness before launch, separately from technical readiness
  • Complete PM-driven launch inputs on time without being chased
  • Define alpha/beta objectives, success criteria, and exit decisions
  • Work with engineering to separate deployment timing from customer rollout timing
  • Keep artifacts current as scope changes throughout the release
  • Ensure PM team is consistently delivering product readiness signoff on time
  • Anticipate launch-impacting risks earlier and surface them before the readiness window
  • Own the release narrative for major features and drive commercial partner alignment
  • Own the zone's release readiness standard across all areas
  • Ensure a consistent, documented separation between technical and product readiness
  • Represent zone readiness in Release Preview and ELT-level launch conversations
What your manager is watching for
  • Do you understand the readiness process before being asked to support it?
  • Can you clearly distinguish between deployment and customer rollout?
  • Are your launch inputs reviewed for accuracy before submission?
  • Are your launch inputs complete and accurate without requiring cleanup?
  • Are you managing alpha/beta logistics effectively?
  • Do you understand PM's readiness ownership vs. engineering's technical ownership?
  • Is the PM delivering readiness signoff on time, or being chased?
  • Is the PM owning product readiness explicitly, not deferring to engineering or PrOps?
  • Are alpha/beta exit criteria clear and defensible before rollout decisions are made?
  • Is the Sr. Manager driving consistent readiness practice across the team?
  • Are major release narratives being communicated effectively to commercial partners?
  • Are launch-impacting risks being surfaced early, not at the last minute?
  • Is the zone's readiness standard consistently applied across all areas?
  • Is the Director representing zone readiness credibly in Release Preview and ELT conversations?
  • Is the separation between technical and product readiness clear and documented zone-wide?

Measurement & Iteration

Measurement & Iteration

PM evaluates performance against success criteria, captures learning, and iterates. Launch is not the end state — it is the handoff into measurement and reprioritization that feeds back into Strategic Planning.

Who owns what
  • PM — Success review, prioritization changes, learning reuse, post-GA outcome ownership
  • Engineering — Root cause, defect resolution, technical follow-through
  • Architecture — Assessment of whether post-launch issues point to architectural root causes, recommendations for structural changes that prevent recurrence
  • Product Ops — Post-GA success services, end-of-life feature services, ongoing roadmap/Jira tooling
  • UX — User feedback interpretation, usability iteration, design refinement
PM focus at your level
  • Learn how success metrics are defined and tracked after launch
  • Participate in post-launch reviews as an observer and note-taker
  • Begin to understand how GA outcomes feed back into Strategic Planning
  • Track adoption and outcome signals for assigned features after launch
  • Contribute to post-launch reviews with data prepared and organized in advance
  • Begin connecting post-launch signal to backlog prioritization for future releases
  • Define success metrics before launch — never after
  • Review GA metrics, adoption signals, and customer feedback at defined intervals
  • Participate in retrospectives so launch learning changes future behavior
  • Convert post-launch signal into updated prioritization decisions that feed back into Strategic Planning
  • Own a portfolio of 5–10 outcome measures across the product area
  • Ensure learning from each launch feeds back into the next strategic planning cycle systematically
  • Drive retrospective quality — move beyond what happened to what changes as a result
  • Own the zone's measurement standard — what outcomes does the zone track and how?
  • Connect outcome data to ELT conversations and external proof points for commercial teams
  • Ensure post-launch learning shapes future zone-level strategic direction
What your manager is watching for
  • Are you developing the habit of asking whether work achieved its intended outcome?
  • Are you attending post-launch reviews and building fluency with outcome measurement?
  • Can you explain how GA outcomes connect to the next planning cycle?
  • Is your post-launch data preparation accurate and complete before reviews?
  • Are you connecting outcome signals to concrete backlog implications?
  • Are you running post-launch reviews that produce actionable decisions, not just summaries?
  • Is the PM defining success metrics before launch, not retrofitting them after?
  • Is post-launch learning actually changing future prioritization decisions?
  • Are retrospective insights producing durable process or roadmap changes?
  • Is the Sr. Manager's portfolio of measures being actively tracked and reported?
  • Is outcome data influencing zone-level strategy, not just team-level backlog?
  • Is learning being captured at the team level and shared across the zone?
  • Is the zone's measurement practice generating strategic insight?
  • Is outcome data being connected to ELT and commercial conversations credibly?
  • Is post-launch learning shaping future zone-level strategic direction?

Continuous Backlog & Bug Governance

PM keeps bugs and enhancements legible, prioritized, and connected to release and roadmap tradeoffs at every stage. This is not a discrete phase — it is an ongoing discipline.

PM owns
  • Backlog health and issue prioritization judgment
  • Enhancement tradeoffs and product-level bug awareness
  • Escalation when defects create roadmap or patient-safety risk
Partners own
  • Engineering — root cause, fix path, delivery estimates
  • Architecture — assessment of whether patterns or pipeline design are contributing to recurring issues
  • PrOps — intake hygiene, visibility, cadence support
  • UX — experience implications for fixes
What good looks like
  • Bug backlog visible in Zone Reviews as a standing item
  • Triage participation is active, not passive
  • Backlog order reflects judgment, not accumulation
Your Onboarding

A structured ramp designed to build strategic PMs, not just process familiarity

The goal is not only to teach tools and ceremonies. It is to build Product Managers who can define a vision for their area, translate that vision into roadmap choices, and align stakeholders around customer workflows and pain points. Your onboarding happens in three distinct layers.

Handled by athena's central onboarding program. Before anything product-related, you'll get set up as an athena employee.

  • Laptop setup and IT access provisioning

  • Benefits, HR policies, and compliance training
  • Security, access controls, and identity setup
  • Understanding athena's mission, values, and organizational structure
  • Introduction to key internal platforms (Workday, Okta, etc.)

Product at Athena — 3-Day Onboarding Program

A structured 3-day program covering how product management works across athena. Run centrally by the Product Division.

  • Support Environments
  • Product Management at athena
Day 2 — March 11
  • Product Knowledge (PK) Overview

Day 3 — March 12

Zone-specific onboarding that happens after Layers 1 and 2. This is where you go deep on the DEP area, its customers, its tools, and how work actually moves.

How to launch

Get into Claude Code — New Hire

Six steps. Takes under 10 minutes the first time.

  1. Open Terminal  Mac: ⌘ Space → type Terminal → Enter
  2. Set up SSH access to Bitbucket (first time only)
    A. Check if you already have an SSH key
    ls ~/.ssh/id_*.pub
    If you see a filename like id_ed25519.pub or id_rsa.pub — you already have a key. Skip to step B below. If you see "No such file or directory" or nothing at all — you need to create one. Continue with the next command.
    Only if you don't have a key: generate one
    ssh-keygen -t ed25519 -C "your-email@athenahealth.com"
    The terminal will ask you three questions. For each one, just press Enter to accept the default:
    Enter file in which to save the key:  → press Enter Enter passphrase (empty for no passphrase):  → press Enter Enter same passphrase again:  → press Enter
    You'll see a message saying your key has been saved. You're done with this part.
    B. Copy your public key
    cat ~/.ssh/id_ed25519.pub | pbcopy
    This copies your key to your clipboard silently — nothing will print, that's normal. If your key file is named id_rsa.pub instead, use that name. You can now paste it anywhere with ⌘V.
    C. Add your key to Bitbucket
    1. Open bitbucket.athenahealth.com in your browser and log in with your athenahealth username and password
    2. Click your profile picture or avatar in the top-right corner of the page
    3. Click Profile from the dropdown menu
    4. In the left sidebar, look for the Security section — click SSH Keys
    5. Click the Add key button
    6. In the Label field, type something like My MacBook so you can identify it later
    7. In the Key field, paste with ⌘V — the key should start with ssh-ed25519 and end with your email address
    8. Click Save
    D. Verify the connection works
    ssh -T git@bitbucket.athenahealth.com -p 7999
    Success looks like: a line mentioning your username, e.g. Welcome to Bitbucket, firstname-lastname! If you see "Permission denied (publickey)": the key wasn't saved correctly in Bitbucket — go back to step C and try again, making sure you pasted the full key. You may see a prompt asking "Are you sure you want to continue connecting?" — type yes and press Enter. This is normal the first time.
  3. Clone the repo (first time only)
    git clone ssh://git@bitbucket.athenahealth.com:7999/~jakeith/pm-onboarding.git ~/pm-onboarding
    Already cloned? Skip steps 2 and 3.
  4. Create your branch — name it after yourself
    cd ~/pm-onboarding && git checkout -b onboarding/[your-name]
    Example: onboarding/sagar-joshi. This keeps your progress separate from everyone else's.
  5. Launch Claude Code
    claude
    Command not found? Run npm install -g @anthropic-ai/claude-code first.
  6. Paste this as your first message — fill in the blanks
    I'm a new PM starting onboarding. My name is [your name], my role is [your role], and my manager is [manager name]. This is my first session.
    Returning? Say "pick up where we left off" instead and Claude will read your state file.

No manual saving needed. At the end of every session, Claude automatically saves your progress and pushes it to the shared repo. Your manager's dashboard updates the next morning.
